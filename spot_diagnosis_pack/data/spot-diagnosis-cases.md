# Spot Diagnosis Cases
Format: image → answer → explanation.

## Case 01: Xanthelasma
**Image:** `images/full/01-xanthelasma.webp`  
**Answer:** Xanthelasma  
**Explanation:**
- Yellowish subcutaneous lipid plaques, classically on the medial upper or lower eyelids.
- Often bilateral and multiple.
- Mainly a cosmetic issue, but check lipid profile/risk factors as it can be associated with hypercholesterolaemia.
- Treatment options include excision, chemical peeling, laser or cryotherapy; recurrence can occur.

## Case 02: Congenital melanocytic naevus
**Image:** `images/full/02-congenital-melanocytic-naevus.webp`  
**Answer:** Congenital melanocytic naevus  
**Explanation:**
- Pigmented congenital naevus involving periocular skin; may be small and uniform or larger/hairy.
- A split or “kissing” naevus can involve both upper and lower eyelids.
- Large congenital melanocytic naevi carry a risk of malignant transformation, so specialist review is important.
- Treatment, when required, is usually complete surgical excision.

## Case 03: Basal cell carcinoma
**Image:** `images/full/03-basal-cell-carcinoma.webp`  
**Answer:** Basal cell carcinoma  
**Explanation:**
- Most common malignant eyelid tumour; typically affects older, fair-skinned patients with chronic sun exposure.
- Classically a shiny pearly nodule with telangiectasia, or a central ulcer with rolled edges.
- Usually slow-growing and locally invasive rather than metastatic.
- Lower lid and medial canthus are common sites; medial canthal lesions have higher orbital/sinus invasion risk.

## Case 04: Squamous cell carcinoma
**Image:** `images/full/04-squamous-cell-carcinoma.webp`  
**Answer:** Squamous cell carcinoma  
**Explanation:**
- Less common than BCC but usually more aggressive.
- May appear as a hyperkeratotic nodule, ulcer, crusted lesion, or cutaneous horn.
- Pearly rolled margins and telangiectasia are less typical than in BCC.
- Important because it can metastasise to regional lymph nodes and may show perineural spread.

## Case 05: Impetigo
**Image:** `images/full/05-impetigo.webp`  
**Answer:** Impetigo  
**Explanation:**
- Superficial bacterial skin infection, usually due to Staphylococcus aureus or Streptococcus pyogenes.
- Common in children and may involve the eyelids when facial skin is affected.
- Starts as erythematous macules or blisters, then ruptures to form golden-yellow “honey-coloured” crusts.
- Highly contagious; treat with topical and sometimes oral antibiotics depending on severity.

## Case 06: Chronic anterior blepharitis
**Image:** `images/full/06-chronic-anterior-blepharitis.webp`  
**Answer:** Chronic anterior blepharitis  
**Explanation:**
- Chronic inflammation affecting the lid margin around the bases of the eyelashes.
- Typical signs include crusting/collarettes at lash bases, red lid margins and ocular irritation.
- Staphylococcal and seborrhoeic forms are common; Demodex can also contribute.
- Management centres on lid hygiene, warm compresses and targeted treatment when inflamed or infected.

## Case 07: Eyelid coloboma
**Image:** `images/full/07-eyelid-coloboma.webp`  
**Answer:** Eyelid coloboma  
**Explanation:**
- Congenital partial- or full-thickness defect of the eyelid due to incomplete eyelid development.
- Upper lid defects classically occur near the junction of the medial and middle thirds.
- Lower lid defects may be associated with systemic syndromes such as Treacher Collins syndrome.
- Management depends on size: small defects may close primarily; larger defects may need grafts or rotation flaps.

## Case 08: Right orbital cellulitis with ophthalmoplegia
**Image:** `images/full/08-right-orbital-cellulitis-with-ophthalmoplegia.webp`  
**Answer:** Right orbital cellulitis with ophthalmoplegia  
**Explanation:**
- Sight- and life-threatening infection posterior to the orbital septum.
- Red flags include fever, painful eye movements, proptosis, ophthalmoplegia, reduced vision, impaired colour vision or RAPD.
- Often follows sinus infection, especially ethmoid sinusitis in children.
- Requires urgent hospital admission, IV antibiotics, imaging and close monitoring of optic nerve function.

## Case 09: Pinguecula
**Image:** `images/full/09-pinguecula.webp`  
**Answer:** Pinguecula  
**Explanation:**
- Yellow-white mound on the bulbar conjunctiva adjacent to the limbus, usually nasal.
- Represents elastotic degeneration of conjunctival stroma, often related to UV/actinic damage.
- Unlike pterygium, it does not grow across the cornea because the limbal barrier remains intact.
- Usually needs no treatment; lubricants or a short steroid course may help if inflamed.

## Case 10: Filamentous fungal keratitis
**Image:** `images/full/10-filamentous-fungal-keratitis.webp`  
**Answer:** Filamentous fungal keratitis  
**Explanation:**
- Fungal corneal infection; diagnosis is often delayed unless suspicion is high.
- Typical appearance is a grey/yellow-white stromal infiltrate with fluffy or feathery margins.
- Satellite lesions, ring infiltrate, endothelial plaque, hypopyon or corneal thinning may occur.
- Treat urgently with antifungal therapy and specialist corneal input.

## Case 11: Marginal keratitis
**Image:** `images/full/11-marginal-keratitis.webp`  
**Answer:** Marginal keratitis  
**Explanation:**
- Peripheral corneal inflammatory reaction, usually a hypersensitivity response to staphylococcal lid disease.
- Shows marginal subepithelial infiltrates separated from the limbus by a clear zone.
- Often associated with adjacent conjunctival hyperaemia and chronic blepharitis.
- Usually culture-negative; treatment is lid hygiene plus topical steroid/antibiotic when appropriate.

## Case 12: Pseudoexfoliation syndrome and glaucoma
**Image:** `images/full/12-pseudoexfoliation-syndrome-glaucoma.webp`  
**Answer:** Pseudoexfoliation syndrome and glaucoma  
**Explanation:**
- Grey-white pseudoexfoliative material is deposited on the lens capsule, zonules, iris and trabecular meshwork.
- A classic sign is a ring or target-like pattern on the anterior lens capsule after pupil dilation.
- It is the commonest cause of secondary open-angle glaucoma.
- Pseudoexfoliation glaucoma can progress faster than primary open-angle glaucoma and needs careful monitoring.
