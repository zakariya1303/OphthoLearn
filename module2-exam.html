Module 2: Ophthalmology examination

A step-by-step OSCE-style approach to the eye examination.

I - Inspection C - Charts B - Blindspot V - Visual fields A - Accommodations R - Reflexes Fr - Fundoscopy

Tip: Remember "I Can't Believe VAR Fr" — click any tile to jump to that section and expand for detail.

Start with safety

Introduce yourself, explain the exam, and position the patient comfortably before you begin.

Work in a sequence

Move from inspection to vision, pupils, movements and fundoscopy rather than jumping around.

Say what you see

Examiners want a clear structure, not perfect recall of every lesion or measurement.

The examination is usually performed in a dimly lit room to maximise pupil dilation and improve visualisation of the fundus. In practice, dilating drops may be used to improve the view of the retina. However, pupil dilation is not routinely expected during undergraduate sessions or examinations.

For exam purposes, you should simply acknowledge that dilation may be helpful if a better view is required.

Example phrasing

"If I were unable to obtain an adequate view, I may consider dilating the pupil with appropriate drops after checking for contraindications, although this would not normally be expected during an undergraduate examination."

Performing the Examination ▸

Positioning

When examining the patient's right eye, hold the ophthalmoscope in your right hand and look through it using your right eye. Likewise, when examining the patient's left eye, use your left hand and left eye. Set lens to 0.

Begin by asking the patient to look at a distant target behind you and keep their gaze fixed there throughout the examination. Explain that you will place your thumb on their eyebrow so you do not bump into one another. Approach at arm's distance, at a 15 degree angle from the patient's temporal side.

Finding the fundus

Initially, identify the red reflex and move slowly towards the eye while maintaining it.

Note that on many models the red reflex is not visible, but often examiners will ask you to continue as if it has been found.

  • Identify a retinal vessel first, rather than searching randomly.
  • Follow the vessel back until you reach the optic disc.
  • Use the vessel as a guide to keep the examination systematic.

I - Inspection ▸

Careful external inspection often gives immediate diagnostic clues. Record laterality and whether findings are acute or chronic.

Look for

  • Ptosis or asymmetry of the lids
  • Red eye or circumlimbal injection
  • Discharge, crusting or lid margin inflammation
  • Ptosis: asymmetric lid droop — consider CN III palsy, myasthenia gravis (fatigable ptosis), or involutional/aponeurotic causes.
  • Red eye / injection: diffuse conjunctival injection suggests conjunctivitis; a ciliary flush or perilimbal injection points to keratitis or anterior uveitis.
  • Discharge & crusting: mucopurulent discharge commonly accompanies bacterial conjunctivitis; lash crusting and lid margin inflammation suggest blepharitis.
  • Proptosis or globe prominence: thyroid eye disease, orbital cellulitis, or orbital masses; assess for restriction of eye movements.
  • Periorbital swelling / erythema: differentiate preseptal (localised, lids only) from orbital cellulitis (pain, proptosis, ophthalmoplegia, systemic features).
  • Lid lesions: stye (hordeolum), chalazion, ectropion/entropion, lid tumours — note location, tenderness, and fluctuation.
  • Other external signs: corneal opacity, corneal ulceration (look for focal whitening), entropion causing lashes rubbing the cornea.

Tip: Inspect with good lighting and ask the patient to look in primary gaze and in the direction of suspected abnormality. Photograph external findings if useful for referral.

C - Charts (Snellen, Ishihara, Near Vision) ▸

Snellen Chart (distance acuity): Test each eye separately with habitual correction first, then without if needed. Record the line read (e.g., 6/6, 6/12) and note if a pinhole improves vision.

What to document

Line read, use of glasses, pinhole response and any severe vision loss terms such as count fingers or hand movement.

  • Use pinhole to distinguish refractive error from pathological causes; improvement suggests refractive problem.
  • Document clearly: count fingers, hand motion, or perception of light (PL) for severe vision loss.

Ishihara Cards (colour vision): Brief screening test for red-green colour blindness. Patient reads numbers in colour plates.

Near Vision (newspaper / Jaeger chart): Test with patient's reading glasses if worn. Offer a newspaper or dedicated near vision card. Record the smallest line clearly read.

Tip: Always state which correction was used. Document acuity for both corrected and uncorrected vision if significantly different.

B - Blindspot ▸

Optional detailed mapping. Most exams do not require formal blindspot assessment; visual field confrontation is usually sufficient.

V - Visual Fields & Neglect ▸

Confrontation testing screens for peripheral field defects. Compare each quadrant between your field and the patient's, asking them to count fingers or describe movement in each area.

  • Test all four quadrants (superior/inferior × nasal/temporal).
  • Screen for hemianopia (loss of one half of field), quadrantanopia, and central field defects.
  • Assess for neglect: patient may fail to acknowledge stimuli on one side despite intact field.

Tip: Use your own visual field as reference; move your examining finger slowly into the patient's field from the periphery and ask when they first see movement.

A - Accommodations (Near Point & Lens Reflex) ▸

Test the patient's ability to focus on near objects (accommodation) and observe for associated pupil constriction and convergence.

  • Ask patient to focus on your finger held at arm's length, then bring it slowly to the nose (~15 cm).
  • Look for pupil constriction (normal) and inward eye movement (convergence).
  • Loss of accommodation with preserved direct pupil response suggests Adie syndrome or diabetic neuropathy.

Tip: Accommodative amplitude decreases with age (presbyopia). This test is less critical in brief OSCE exams but worth mentioning for complete CN II/III assessment.

R - Reflexes (Direct, Consensual & RAPD) ▸

Inspect pupil size, symmetry, shape and reactivity in room light and dim conditions. Perform direct, consensual and swinging light tests (RAPD assessment).

  • Direct reflex: shine light into one eye; observe pupil constriction in that eye.
  • Consensual reflex: light in one eye causes pupil constriction in the other eye.
  • RAPD (swinging flashlight test): indicates asymmetric afferent pathway function (optic nerve or severe retinal disease on the affected side); the affected pupil dilates paradoxically when light swings to it.
  • Anisocoria: note whether asymmetry is greater in light or dark — helps differentiate parasympathetic vs sympathetic causes.

Tip: Perform RAPD assessment in a slightly dim room with a bright light source; a true RAPD is a critical red flag.

Fr - Fundoscopy (Red Reflex → Disc → Retina) ▸

Red Reflex: The first step of fundoscopy. Shine the ophthalmoscope light at the pupil from arm's length (~30 cm away). Look for a bright red/orange reflex in both eyes. Absence or dimness suggests media opacity (cataract, corneal scar, vitreous haemorrhage) or retinal detachment.

Quick sequence

Red reflex, vessel, disc, macula and then the four retinal quadrants.

Once the red reflex is confirmed, slowly approach the eye and begin your systematic examination:

  • Use your right eye to examine the patient's right eye (right hand with scope); left eye for left eye.
  • Start at lens 0 and adjust as needed to focus the image.
  • Follow a retinal vessel towards the optic disc — this is often easier than searching randomly.
  • Systematically assess the disc, macula, and four retinal quadrants.

Tip: If you cannot visualise the fundus adequately, say so honestly — this is better than inventing findings.

Recognising Common Fundoscopic Findings

To visualise what these fundoscopic findings look like in practice, head to Module 4: Spot Diagnoses, which contains plenty of real examples for you to delve into.

Flame Haemorrhages

Flame-shaped haemorrhages follow the retinal nerve fibre layer and often appear as elongated red streaks. They may be seen in hypertensive retinopathy, retinal vein occlusion, and papilledema.

Dot and Blot Haemorrhages

These appear as small, rounded retinal haemorrhages and are classically associated with diabetic retinopathy.

Hard Exudates

Hard exudates appear as yellow-white deposits with well-defined borders. They represent lipid leakage from damaged retinal vessels and are commonly seen in diabetic retinopathy and hypertensive retinopathy.

Cotton Wool Spots

Cotton wool spots are fluffy white lesions representing focal retinal infarction. They may occur in diabetes, hypertension, retinal vascular disease, and papilledema.

Papilledema

Papilledema describes optic disc swelling secondary to raised intracranial pressure. Features may include blurred disc margins and disc elevation.

Glaucoma

Fundoscopy may reveal: increased cup-to-disc ratio, thinning of the neuroretinal rim and optic disc cupping.

Diabetic Retinopathy

Common findings include microaneurysms, dot and blot haemorrhages, hard exudates, cotton wool spots and neovascularisation.

Clinical Pearls

Many students become frustrated because direct fundoscopy is technically difficult. This is completely normal. Even experienced clinicians sometimes struggle to obtain an ideal view.

Remember that a systematic approach is far more important than speed.

Think: Red Reflex → Vessel → Optic Disc → Four Quadrants → Macula

Following a vessel to the optic disc is often the single most useful practical tip for beginners.

OSCE Tip

In undergraduate examinations, you are rarely expected to identify every retinal lesion perfectly. Examiners are primarily assessing safety, technique, systematic approach and ability to recognise obvious pathology.

Many teaching models do not produce a realistic red reflex and may not allow visualisation of retinal structures. Never invent findings. If you cannot visualise the fundus adequately, say so honestly.

A strong candidate might say:

"I am unable to adequately visualise the retina on this model. If this were a real patient, I would assess the optic disc, cup-to-disc ratio, retinal vessels, all four retinal quadrants and the macula in a systematic manner."
