Retinal Detachment

Definition

Separation of the neurosensory retina from the underlying retinal pigment epithelium.

Symptoms

Flashes, floaters, a curtain or shadow across vision, and visual loss.

Signs

Detached retina, retinal tears and reduced visual acuity.

Management

Urgent surgical repair.

Referral

Same-day ophthalmology referral.

Retinal detachment is a sight-threatening condition in which the neurosensory retina separates from the underlying retinal pigment epithelium. This separation disrupts the retina's blood supply and metabolic support, resulting in progressive visual loss.

Early recognition is crucial because prompt treatment can preserve vision. Delayed diagnosis may result in permanent visual impairment, particularly if the macula becomes detached.

Retinal detachment is considered an ophthalmic emergency and should always be assessed urgently.

History of the Disease

For many centuries retinal detachment was considered untreatable and almost invariably resulted in blindness. During the nineteenth century, advances in ophthalmoscopy enabled clinicians to visualise retinal tears and detachments directly for the first time.

A major breakthrough occurred in the early twentieth century when Jules Gonin recognised that retinal tears were responsible for many detachments. His pioneering surgical techniques dramatically improved outcomes and laid the foundation for modern retinal surgery.

Today, advances such as vitrectomy, scleral buckling and pneumatic retinopexy allow successful treatment in the vast majority of patients.

Timeline

  • 1851 - Helmholtz invents the ophthalmoscope
  • 1920s - Jules Gonin identifies retinal tears as the cause of many detachments
  • 1950s - Scleral buckling becomes established
  • 1970s - Vitrectomy introduced
  • Present day - Highly successful retinal surgery available

Epidemiology

Retinal detachment affects approximately 1 in 10,000 people annually and becomes more common with advancing age. Most cases occur between the ages of 50 and 70 years.

Although relatively uncommon compared with cataracts or glaucoma, retinal detachment remains one of the most important ophthalmic emergencies due to its potential to cause irreversible visual loss.

Risk Factors

The strongest risk factor is posterior vitreous detachment, a common age-related change in which the vitreous separates from the retina. During this process, traction may create retinal tears that subsequently allow fluid to pass beneath the retina.

Additional risk factors include myopia, previous retinal detachment, ocular trauma, cataract surgery, lattice degeneration and a family history of retinal detachment.

Patients who have experienced retinal detachment in one eye are at increased risk of developing it in the fellow eye.

Pathophysiology

The retina normally remains attached to the underlying retinal pigment epithelium through a combination of hydrostatic forces and active transport mechanisms.

When a retinal tear develops, liquefied vitreous can pass through the defect and accumulate beneath the retina. This separates the neurosensory retina from its supporting tissues and disrupts photoreceptor function.

Without treatment, the area of detachment typically enlarges, leading to progressive visual loss.

Classification

Retinal detachment is generally classified into three main types.

Rhegmatogenous

The most common type. A retinal tear allows fluid to enter the subretinal space.

Tractional

Fibrovascular membranes pull the retina away from the retinal pigment epithelium, commonly in proliferative diabetic retinopathy.

Exudative

Fluid accumulates beneath the retina without a retinal tear. Causes include inflammatory disease, tumours and severe hypertension.

Symptoms

Patients classically describe the sudden onset of flashes and floaters. Flashes occur because vitreoretinal traction stimulates retinal photoreceptors, while floaters often result from vitreous degeneration or bleeding.

As the detachment progresses, patients frequently report a dark curtain or shadow moving across their visual field. The location of the shadow corresponds to the area of detached retina.

Visual acuity may remain normal initially if the macula remains attached. However, once the macula becomes involved, central vision deteriorates rapidly.

Signs

Examination may reveal

  • Reduced visual acuity
  • Visual field defects
  • Relative afferent pupillary defect in extensive detachments
  • Elevated, corrugated retina
  • Retinal tears or holes
  • Pigment cells within the vitreous ("tobacco dust")

Investigations

Diagnosis is usually made clinically through dilated retinal examination.

Additional investigations may include:

  • Visual acuity assessment
  • Dilated fundoscopy
  • Optical coherence tomography (OCT)
  • B-scan ultrasonography when the fundus view is obscured

Ultrasound is particularly useful when vitreous haemorrhage prevents direct visualisation of the retina.

Management

Management depends upon the type and extent of detachment.

Small retinal tears without detachment may be treated with laser photocoagulation or cryotherapy to prevent progression.

Established retinal detachments generally require surgical intervention. Treatment options include pneumatic retinopexy, scleral buckling and pars plana vitrectomy.

Visual outcomes are significantly better when treatment occurs before the macula detaches.

Complications

Untreated retinal detachment can result in permanent visual loss. Even after successful repair, some patients experience persistent distortion, reduced visual acuity or recurrent retinal detachment.

Proliferative vitreoretinopathy represents a particularly challenging complication and is a major cause of surgical failure.

Red Flags & Referral Guidance

Same-day ophthalmology referral is required for:

  • New flashes and floaters
  • A curtain or shadow across vision
  • Sudden visual field loss
  • Suspected retinal tear
  • Suspected retinal detachment

Any patient describing a "curtain coming down over the eye" should be assumed to have a retinal detachment until proven otherwise.

Clinical Pearl

Flashes and floaters are common, but flashes and floaters accompanied by a visual field defect are much more concerning and should prompt urgent retinal assessment.

OSCE Tip

When taking a history of sudden visual loss, always ask specifically about flashes, floaters, curtains or shadows, previous retinal detachment and high myopia.

  • Flashes
  • Floaters
  • Curtains or shadows
  • Previous retinal detachment
  • High myopia

These questions help distinguish retinal detachment from other causes of visual loss.

Key Takeaway

Retinal detachment is a sight-threatening ophthalmic emergency characterised by flashes, floaters and a curtain-like visual field defect. Early diagnosis and urgent surgical treatment are essential for preserving vision, particularly before the macula becomes detached.

Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma

Definition

Sudden closure of the anterior chamber angle causing rapid elevation of intraocular pressure.

Symptoms

Severe eye pain, blurred vision, headache, halos around lights, nausea and vomiting.

Signs

Red eye, cloudy cornea, fixed mid-dilated pupil, markedly elevated intraocular pressure.

Management

Immediate pressure reduction followed by laser peripheral iridotomy.

Referral

Same-day ophthalmology emergency.

Acute angle-closure glaucoma (AACG) is one of the true ophthalmic emergencies. It occurs when the drainage angle between the iris and cornea suddenly closes, preventing aqueous humour from leaving the eye. As intraocular pressure rises rapidly, the optic nerve becomes compromised and permanent visual loss may occur if treatment is delayed.

Unlike primary open-angle glaucoma, which develops slowly over many years, acute angle-closure glaucoma presents suddenly and dramatically. Prompt recognition and urgent treatment are essential to preserve vision.

History of the Disease

For centuries, physicians recognised that some patients developed sudden blindness associated with an extremely painful and hardened eye. However, the mechanism remained unclear until advances in ophthalmic anatomy during the nineteenth century.

The development of gonioscopy allowed clinicians to visualise the anterior chamber angle directly and understand the relationship between angle closure and raised intraocular pressure. The introduction of laser peripheral iridotomy during the twentieth century transformed management, allowing definitive treatment without major surgery.

Timeline

  • 1851 - Helmholtz invents the ophthalmoscope
  • Late 1800s - Development of tonometry
  • Early 1900s - Gonioscopy allows visualisation of the angle
  • 1970s - Laser peripheral iridotomy introduced
  • Present day - Rapid diagnosis and highly effective treatment

Epidemiology

Acute angle-closure glaucoma is significantly less common than primary open-angle glaucoma but carries a much greater risk of rapid visual loss. It occurs most frequently in older adults, particularly women and individuals with anatomically narrow anterior chamber angles.

The condition is especially prevalent in East Asian populations and in patients with hypermetropia (long-sightedness).

Risk Factors

The most important risk factor is a naturally narrow anterior chamber angle. Hypermetropic eyes tend to be shorter, leaving less space within the anterior chamber and increasing the likelihood of angle closure.

Other important risk factors include increasing age, female sex, family history of angle-closure glaucoma and cataract formation. Certain medications that cause pupillary dilation may precipitate an attack in susceptible individuals.

Pathophysiology

Normally, aqueous humour produced by the ciliary body flows from the posterior chamber, through the pupil and into the anterior chamber before draining through the trabecular meshwork.

In acute angle closure, aqueous humour becomes trapped behind the iris, creating a pressure gradient known as a pupillary block. This pushes the peripheral iris forwards, causing closure of the drainage angle. Once the angle closes, aqueous humour can no longer drain effectively and intraocular pressure rises rapidly.

Intraocular pressure may exceed 50-60 mmHg, resulting in corneal oedema, optic nerve ischaemia and severe pain.

Symptoms

Patients classically present with the sudden onset of severe ocular pain, often described as deep and intense. Vision becomes blurred and patients frequently report seeing coloured halos around lights due to corneal oedema.

The pain may be accompanied by headache, nausea and vomiting, leading some patients to initially present to emergency departments or general medical services rather than ophthalmology clinics.

Symptoms often develop in the evening or in dim lighting when physiological pupillary dilation occurs.

Signs

Examination may reveal

  • Markedly reduced visual acuity
  • Diffuse conjunctival injection
  • Corneal oedema causing a cloudy appearance
  • Fixed mid-dilated pupil
  • Shallow anterior chamber
  • Elevated intraocular pressure
  • Tender, firm globe on gentle palpation

Investigations

Diagnosis is primarily clinical but supported by:

  • Visual acuity assessment
  • Tonometry
  • Slit-lamp examination
  • Gonioscopy
  • Optic nerve assessment following pressure reduction

A markedly elevated intraocular pressure in the presence of a painful red eye is highly suggestive of acute angle closure.

Management

Treatment aims to rapidly reduce intraocular pressure and prevent permanent optic nerve damage.

Initial management typically includes topical beta-blockers, alpha agonists and systemic acetazolamide. Once intraocular pressure begins to fall, pilocarpine may be administered to constrict the pupil and relieve pupillary block.

Definitive treatment is achieved with laser peripheral iridotomy, which creates a small opening in the peripheral iris, allowing aqueous humour to bypass the blockage. Because the fellow eye is often anatomically predisposed to angle closure, prophylactic treatment is frequently recommended.

Complications

Without urgent treatment, acute angle-closure glaucoma can lead to irreversible optic nerve damage and permanent blindness. Recurrent attacks may occur if definitive treatment is not performed.

Persistent elevation of intraocular pressure can also result in chronic glaucoma and progressive visual field loss.

Red Flags & Referral Guidance

Any patient presenting with a painful red eye and reduced vision should be assessed urgently.

Immediate ophthalmology referral is required when any of the following are present:

  • Severe eye pain
  • Sudden visual deterioration
  • Fixed mid-dilated pupil
  • Corneal clouding
  • Nausea or vomiting accompanying ocular symptoms
  • Markedly elevated intraocular pressure

Acute angle-closure glaucoma should always be considered a same-day emergency.

Clinical Pearl

A painful red eye associated with nausea and vomiting is acute angle-closure glaucoma until proven otherwise.

Many students incorrectly diagnose conjunctivitis because both conditions present with redness, but conjunctivitis does not cause severe pain, reduced vision or a fixed pupil.

OSCE Tip

When examining a painful red eye, always comment on:

  • Visual acuity
  • Corneal clarity
  • Pupil size and reactivity
  • Presence of photophobia

Mentioning a fixed mid-dilated pupil is a high-yield examination finding and often earns additional marks.

Key Takeaway

Acute angle-closure glaucoma is a sight-threatening ophthalmic emergency caused by sudden obstruction of aqueous humour drainage. Patients typically present with severe eye pain, blurred vision, headache and nausea. Prompt recognition and immediate ophthalmology referral are essential to prevent permanent blindness.

Keratitis

Definition

Inflammation of the cornea, most commonly caused by infection.

Symptoms

Painful red eye, photophobia, watering and reduced vision.

Signs

Corneal opacity, epithelial defects and conjunctival injection.

Management

Depends on the cause but often requires urgent ophthalmology assessment and antimicrobial therapy.

Referral

Same-day ophthalmology referral.

Keratitis refers to inflammation of the cornea and represents one of the most important causes of a painful red eye. Although inflammation may occur secondary to trauma, dry eye disease or autoimmune conditions, infectious keratitis is particularly significant because it can rapidly threaten vision.

The cornea normally remains transparent due to its highly organised collagen structure. Inflammation disrupts this arrangement, resulting in corneal oedema, scarring and visual impairment. Prompt recognition is therefore essential.

Contact lens wear remains one of the most important risk factors for infectious keratitis in developed countries.

History of the Disease

Corneal disease has been recognised since antiquity, but understanding improved dramatically following the invention of the slit lamp in the early twentieth century. The ability to visualise the corneal epithelium and stroma allowed clinicians to distinguish different forms of keratitis and understand their underlying causes.

The discovery of antimicrobial therapies transformed outcomes, converting many previously blinding infections into treatable conditions. More recently, advances in microbiology and corneal imaging have improved diagnosis and management.

Timeline

  • 1851 - Ophthalmoscope introduced
  • 1911 - Slit lamp biomicroscopy developed
  • 1940s - Antibiotics become available
  • 1980s - Contact lens-related keratitis increasingly recognised
  • Present day - Advanced corneal imaging and targeted antimicrobial therapy

Epidemiology

Keratitis occurs worldwide and remains a significant cause of visual impairment. Incidence varies according to geography and underlying risk factors. In developed countries, contact lens wear is a leading cause of microbial keratitis. In developing regions, ocular trauma and agricultural injuries are more commonly implicated.

Although many cases are successfully treated, delayed diagnosis remains an important cause of preventable blindness.

Risk Factors

Contact lens wear is the most important risk factor for microbial keratitis. Poor lens hygiene, overnight lens use and exposure of lenses to water substantially increase the risk.

Additional risk factors include ocular trauma, previous corneal disease, dry eye syndrome, immunosuppression and chronic corticosteroid use. Herpes simplex infection is a particularly important cause of recurrent keratitis.

Pathophysiology

The corneal epithelium serves as a barrier against infection. When this barrier is disrupted through trauma, contact lens wear or pre-existing disease, microorganisms may gain access to deeper corneal layers.

The resulting inflammatory response leads to infiltration of immune cells, tissue damage and corneal oedema. If untreated, progressive destruction of corneal tissue may occur, resulting in ulceration, perforation and permanent scarring.

Classification

Keratitis may be broadly divided into infectious and non-infectious causes.

Infectious

Bacterial, viral, fungal or protozoal. Bacterial keratitis is most common; acanthamoeba is associated with contact lens use and water exposure.

Non-infectious

May occur secondary to dry eye disease, autoimmune disorders or exposure-related corneal damage.

Symptoms

Patients typically present with a painful red eye accompanied by photophobia and excessive tearing. Many describe a sensation of having something in the eye and may struggle to keep the affected eye open.

Visual acuity is often reduced, particularly when the central cornea is involved. Symptoms usually develop rapidly and tend to be significantly more severe than those seen in simple conjunctivitis.

Signs

Examination may reveal

  • Conjunctival injection
  • Corneal opacity
  • Corneal ulceration
  • Epithelial defects
  • Photophobia
  • Reduced visual acuity
  • Hypopyon in severe infection

Investigations

Assessment begins with visual acuity testing and slit lamp examination. Fluorescein staining is particularly useful because it highlights epithelial defects and corneal ulcers.

Additional investigations may include:

  • Visual acuity assessment
  • Fluorescein staining
  • Slit lamp examination
  • Corneal scraping for microscopy and culture
  • Corneal imaging

Management

Management depends upon the underlying cause. Infectious keratitis generally requires urgent antimicrobial treatment and close ophthalmic follow-up.

Bacterial keratitis is commonly treated with intensive topical antibiotics. Viral keratitis may require antiviral therapy, whereas fungal and protozoal infections require specialised treatment.

Because progression can be rapid, suspected infectious keratitis should always be considered an ophthalmic emergency.

Complications

Delayed treatment may result in corneal scarring, perforation and permanent visual loss. Severe infection can occasionally progress to endophthalmitis, a devastating intraocular infection.

Even after successful treatment, residual corneal scarring may significantly affect vision.

Red Flags & Referral Guidance

Urgent ophthalmology assessment is required for:

  • Contact lens wearer with a painful red eye
  • Reduced visual acuity
  • Corneal opacity
  • Corneal ulceration
  • Severe photophobia

Any contact lens wearer with a painful red eye should be assumed to have microbial keratitis until proven otherwise.

Clinical Pearl

Pain, photophobia and reduced vision should immediately raise suspicion for keratitis rather than conjunctivitis.

OSCE Tip

When assessing a red eye, always ask whether the patient wears contact lenses. This is one of the highest-yield questions in ophthalmology examinations.

Key Takeaway

Keratitis is a potentially sight-threatening cause of a painful red eye. Contact lens wear is the most important risk factor, and urgent ophthalmology assessment is required to prevent permanent visual loss.

Uveitis

Definition

Inflammation of the uveal tract, most commonly affecting the anterior chamber.

Symptoms

Painful red eye, photophobia and blurred vision.

Signs

Circumlimbal redness, inflammatory cells in the anterior chamber and a constricted pupil.

Management

Topical corticosteroids and cycloplegic agents under specialist supervision.

Referral

Urgent ophthalmology referral.

Uveitis refers to inflammation of the uveal tract, which consists of the iris, ciliary body and choroid. Although inflammation may occur in any of these structures, anterior uveitis is by far the most common form encountered in clinical practice.

The condition is important because it can lead to significant visual impairment if untreated. It is also strongly associated with systemic inflammatory disease, meaning recognition may provide clues to an underlying diagnosis.

History of the Disease

Descriptions of painful red eyes associated with intraocular inflammation date back centuries. However, understanding improved substantially with the development of slit lamp biomicroscopy, which allowed clinicians to visualise inflammatory cells within the anterior chamber.

The discovery of corticosteroids in the mid-twentieth century revolutionised management and dramatically reduced the visual complications associated with severe inflammation.

Timeline

  • 1851 - Ophthalmoscope introduced
  • 1911 - Slit lamp biomicroscopy developed
  • 1940s - Corticosteroids introduced
  • Late 1900s - Improved understanding of autoimmune associations
  • Present day - Advanced immunomodulatory therapy available

Epidemiology

Uveitis accounts for a significant proportion of visual impairment in working-age adults. Although it may occur at any age, anterior uveitis most commonly affects younger and middle-aged adults.

A substantial proportion of cases are associated with underlying systemic inflammatory disease, although many remain idiopathic.

Risk Factors

Anterior uveitis is strongly associated with HLA-B27-related conditions, including ankylosing spondylitis, reactive arthritis, psoriatic arthritis and inflammatory bowel disease.

Other associations include sarcoidosis, Behçet disease, juvenile idiopathic arthritis and various infectious conditions.

A previous episode of uveitis significantly increases the likelihood of recurrence.

Pathophysiology

Inflammation within the uveal tract leads to breakdown of the blood-aqueous barrier and migration of inflammatory cells into the anterior chamber.

These inflammatory cells and proteins disrupt normal ocular function and may result in pain, photophobia and visual disturbance. Chronic inflammation can cause structural damage, including cataract formation and secondary glaucoma.

Symptoms

Patients typically present with a painful red eye accompanied by marked photophobia. Vision is often blurred and many individuals describe discomfort when exposed to bright light.

Symptoms generally develop over hours to days and may affect one or both eyes.

Signs

Examination may reveal

  • Circumlimbal redness (ciliary flush)
  • Constricted pupil
  • Irregular pupil due to posterior synechiae
  • Cells and flare in the anterior chamber
  • Reduced visual acuity
  • Photophobia

Investigations

Diagnosis is primarily clinical and relies upon slit lamp examination.

Additional investigations may include:

  • Visual acuity assessment
  • Slit lamp examination
  • Intraocular pressure measurement
  • Blood tests guided by clinical suspicion
  • Chest imaging where indicated

Investigation for systemic disease may be appropriate in recurrent or severe cases.

Management

Treatment aims to reduce inflammation, relieve symptoms and prevent complications. Topical corticosteroids remain the cornerstone of therapy and are often combined with cycloplegic agents to reduce pain and prevent synechiae formation.

Patients with recurrent or severe disease may require systemic immunosuppression under specialist supervision.

Complications

Untreated uveitis can result in cataract formation, glaucoma, cystoid macular oedema and permanent visual loss. Recurrent disease increases the likelihood of long-term complications.

Prompt treatment significantly improves visual outcomes.

Red Flags & Referral Guidance

Urgent ophthalmology referral is required for:

  • Painful photophobic red eye
  • Reduced visual acuity
  • Suspected anterior uveitis
  • Recurrent episodes of ocular inflammation

Clinical Pearl

Photophobia is one of the most important clues to uveitis and should never be ignored when assessing a red eye.

OSCE Tip

When taking a history, always ask about back pain, psoriasis and inflammatory bowel disease. These associations are frequently tested in examinations.

Key Takeaway

Anterior uveitis is a common cause of a painful photophobic red eye and is frequently associated with systemic inflammatory disease. Early diagnosis and treatment are essential to prevent sight-threatening complications.

Scleritis

Definition

A severe inflammatory disorder affecting the sclera.

Symptoms

Severe deep ocular pain, redness and reduced vision.

Signs

Violaceous scleral injection, tenderness and scleral inflammation.

Management

Systemic anti-inflammatory treatment and investigation for underlying disease.

Referral

Urgent ophthalmology referral.

Scleritis is a potentially sight-threatening inflammatory condition affecting the sclera, the dense connective tissue forming the outer wall of the eye. Unlike episcleritis, which is usually benign and self-limiting, scleritis is often associated with significant pain and systemic autoimmune disease.

The condition requires prompt recognition because delayed treatment can result in permanent visual impairment and, in severe cases, scleral thinning or perforation.

History of the Disease

Historically, severe ocular inflammation involving the sclera was poorly understood and often grouped with other causes of red eye. The development of slit lamp biomicroscopy enabled clinicians to distinguish deep scleral inflammation from more superficial episcleral disease.

During the twentieth century, the strong association between scleritis and autoimmune diseases such as rheumatoid arthritis became increasingly recognised. This shifted management from purely local treatment to a multidisciplinary approach involving rheumatology and immunology.

Timeline

  • 1911 - Slit lamp biomicroscopy introduced
  • Mid-1900s - Association with autoimmune disease recognised
  • 1970s - Systemic immunosuppressive therapy becomes established
  • Present day - Multidisciplinary management improves outcomes

Epidemiology

Scleritis is significantly less common than episcleritis but carries a much greater risk of visual morbidity. It most commonly affects middle-aged adults and is more frequent in women.

A substantial proportion of patients have an underlying systemic inflammatory condition, particularly rheumatoid arthritis.

Risk Factors

The strongest risk factor is underlying autoimmune disease. Rheumatoid arthritis is the most commonly associated condition, although granulomatosis with polyangiitis, systemic lupus erythematosus, inflammatory bowel disease and relapsing polychondritis may also occur in association.

Previous episodes of scleritis increase the likelihood of recurrence.

Pathophysiology

Scleritis results from inflammation of the deep vascular plexus within the sclera. Immune-mediated damage leads to infiltration of inflammatory cells and progressive destruction of scleral tissue.

In severe disease, chronic inflammation can cause scleral thinning, exposing the underlying uveal tissue and increasing the risk of structural complications.

Symptoms

Patients typically describe severe, deep and boring ocular pain. The pain often radiates to the forehead, jaw or temple and may be severe enough to disturb sleep. Eye movements frequently exacerbate discomfort.

Many patients also experience photophobia, watering and reduced vision. Symptoms tend to be substantially more severe than those seen in episcleritis.

Signs

Examination may reveal

  • Diffuse or sectoral redness
  • Violaceous hue to the sclera
  • Severe tenderness
  • Reduced visual acuity
  • Scleral thinning in advanced disease
  • Associated keratitis or uveitis

Investigations

Diagnosis is primarily clinical but should prompt investigation for underlying systemic disease.

  • Visual acuity assessment
  • Slit lamp examination
  • ESR and CRP
  • Autoimmune screen
  • Rheumatology assessment where appropriate

Management

Management depends on severity and underlying cause. Mild cases may respond to oral NSAIDs, but many patients require systemic corticosteroids or immunosuppressive therapy.

Close collaboration with rheumatology is often necessary when autoimmune disease is identified.

Complications

Untreated scleritis can result in scleral thinning, cataract formation, secondary glaucoma, keratitis and permanent visual loss.

Necrotising scleritis is particularly aggressive and can threaten both vision and ocular integrity.

Red Flags & Referral Guidance

Urgent ophthalmology referral is required for:

  • Severe ocular pain
  • Reduced vision
  • Violaceous scleral redness
  • Suspected scleritis in a patient with autoimmune disease

Clinical Pearl

Severe deep pain is the feature that most strongly points toward scleritis.

OSCE Tip

If asked to differentiate episcleritis and scleritis, emphasise pain, visual loss and systemic autoimmune associations.

Key Takeaway

Scleritis is a severe inflammatory disorder of the sclera that is frequently associated with systemic autoimmune disease. It presents with severe pain and requires urgent specialist assessment.

Papilloedema

Definition

Bilateral optic disc swelling caused by raised intracranial pressure.

Symptoms

Headache, transient visual obscurations, nausea, vomiting and pulsatile tinnitus.

Signs

Swollen optic discs with blurred margins, haemorrhages and loss of venous pulsation.

Management

Urgent neuroimaging and treatment of the underlying cause.

Referral

Urgent ophthalmology and neurology assessment.

Papilloedema is optic disc swelling caused by raised intracranial pressure. It is an important neurological sign because it may indicate serious intracranial pathology such as a mass lesion, hydrocephalus or cerebral venous sinus thrombosis.

Although the finding is detected on eye examination, the underlying problem is neurological. Recognition is therefore critical for both vision and life-saving diagnosis.

History of the Disease

The association between optic disc swelling and intracranial disease became clearer after the ophthalmoscope was introduced in the nineteenth century. This allowed clinicians to directly visualise the optic nerve head and identify papilloedema as a sign of raised intracranial pressure.

Modern imaging has refined diagnosis, but the core clinical principle remains unchanged: papilloedema is a red flag for intracranial pathology.

Timeline

  • 1851 - Helmholtz invents the ophthalmoscope
  • Late 1800s - Association with raised intracranial pressure recognised
  • 1970s - CT scanning improves diagnosis of intracranial causes
  • 1980s - MRI provides better soft tissue imaging
  • Present day - Combined neuroimaging and ophthalmic assessment guide care

Symptoms

Patients often present with headache, transient visual obscurations, nausea, vomiting and pulsatile tinnitus. The visual symptoms may be brief and positional, such as momentary dimming when standing up or bending over.

Visual acuity may remain normal in the early stages, so a normal acuity test does not rule out papilloedema.

Signs

Examination may reveal

  • Bilateral optic disc swelling
  • Blurred disc margins
  • Venous congestion
  • Peripapillary haemorrhages
  • Absence of spontaneous venous pulsation

Investigations

The first priority is to identify the cause of raised intracranial pressure.

  • Visual acuity and colour vision
  • Formal visual fields
  • Fundoscopy and OCT
  • CT or MRI brain
  • MR venography if venous sinus thrombosis is suspected
  • Lumbar puncture after imaging when appropriate

Management

Management depends on the underlying cause. Treatment may include neurosurgical intervention, treatment of infection, management of venous thrombosis or lowering intracranial pressure in idiopathic intracranial hypertension.

Regular monitoring is important because persistent papilloedema can progress to optic atrophy and permanent visual loss.

Red Flags & Referral Guidance

Urgent assessment is required for new optic disc swelling, headache with visual symptoms, diplopia, pulsatile tinnitus or any suspicion of raised intracranial pressure.

Clinical Pearl

Papilloedema is usually bilateral and may be present even when visual acuity is normal.

Key Takeaway

Papilloedema is bilateral optic disc swelling caused by raised intracranial pressure and should always trigger urgent investigation for a potentially serious intracranial cause.

Ocular Trauma

Definition

Injury to the eye or surrounding structures resulting from blunt, penetrating or chemical mechanisms.

Symptoms

Pain, redness, reduced vision and photophobia.

Signs

Variable depending on injury severity.

Management

Depends on mechanism and severity.

Referral

Urgent ophthalmology assessment.

Ocular trauma encompasses a wide spectrum of injuries ranging from minor corneal abrasions to devastating globe ruptures. It remains a major cause of preventable visual impairment worldwide and requires prompt assessment to identify sight-threatening injuries.

The priority is always preservation of vision and identification of emergencies that require immediate specialist intervention.

History of the Disease

Advances in microsurgery, imaging and ocular reconstruction have dramatically improved outcomes for patients with eye injuries. However, prevention remains the most effective strategy, particularly through the use of protective eyewear.

Timeline

  • 1800s - Early ocular surgery develops
  • 1900s - Improved repair techniques emerge
  • 1970s - Modern microsurgery introduced
  • Present day - Advanced imaging and vitreoretinal surgery available

Epidemiology

Ocular trauma affects individuals of all ages but is particularly common among young adults and those involved in manual occupations or contact sports.

Many injuries are preventable through appropriate eye protection.

Risk Factors

Occupational exposure, contact sports, assault and failure to use protective eyewear all increase risk.

Children and young adults account for a significant proportion of injuries.

Classification

Ocular trauma may be classified as:

  • Blunt trauma
  • Penetrating trauma
  • Perforating trauma
  • Chemical injury
  • Thermal injury

Understanding the mechanism often provides important clues regarding severity.

Symptoms

Patients may present with pain, redness, tearing, photophobia or reduced vision. Severe injuries may result in sudden visual loss.

The history should always establish exactly how the injury occurred.

Signs

Examination may reveal

  • Corneal abrasion
  • Hyphaema
  • Irregular pupil
  • Reduced visual acuity
  • Globe rupture
  • Lid laceration

Investigations

Assessment should include:

  • Visual acuity testing
  • Pupil examination
  • Slit lamp assessment
  • Fluorescein staining
  • CT imaging where indicated

Visual acuity should always be documented.

Management

Management depends on the underlying injury. Minor abrasions may require lubrication and antibiotic prophylaxis, whereas open globe injuries require emergency surgical repair.

Suspected globe rupture should never undergo pressure measurement.

Complications

Complications include infection, glaucoma, cataract formation, retinal detachment and permanent visual loss.

Red Flags & Referral Guidance

Immediate ophthalmology referral is required for:

  • Reduced vision
  • Hyphaema
  • Suspected globe rupture
  • Penetrating injury
  • Chemical injury
  • Irregular pupil

Clinical Pearl

Do not apply pressure to a suspected open globe injury.

OSCE Tip

In any eye injury station, your first step should be documenting visual acuity.

Key Takeaway

Ocular trauma ranges from minor injuries to sight-threatening emergencies. Early recognition, accurate assessment and urgent referral are essential for preserving vision.

Optic Neuritis

Definition

Inflammation of the optic nerve causing acute visual loss.

Symptoms

Painful visual loss and colour desaturation.

Signs

Relative afferent pupillary defect and reduced colour vision.

Management

Neurological and ophthalmological assessment.

Referral

Urgent specialist referral.

Optic neuritis is an inflammatory disorder of the optic nerve and is one of the most important neuro-ophthalmological conditions encountered in clinical practice. It is strongly associated with multiple sclerosis and may represent the first manifestation of demyelinating disease.

Prompt recognition is important because the diagnosis has significant implications for future neurological health.

Epidemiology

Optic neuritis most commonly affects young adults between 20 and 45 years of age and is more common in women.

Many patients ultimately develop multiple sclerosis.

Risk Factors

The strongest association is with multiple sclerosis. Other causes include neuromyelitis optica, MOG antibody disease and viral infections.

Pathophysiology

Inflammation damages the myelin sheath surrounding optic nerve fibres, impairing visual signal transmission.

This results in visual loss and characteristic colour vision abnormalities.

Symptoms

Patients usually present with subacute unilateral visual loss developing over several days. Pain, particularly on eye movement, is a classic feature.

Many patients report that colours appear washed out, especially the colour red.

Signs

Examination may reveal

  • Reduced visual acuity
  • Reduced colour vision
  • Relative afferent pupillary defect
  • Visual field defects
  • Optic disc swelling (sometimes)

Investigations

  • Visual acuity testing
  • Colour vision testing
  • Visual field assessment
  • MRI brain and orbits
  • OCT

Management

Management involves urgent ophthalmology and neurology assessment. Intravenous corticosteroids may accelerate visual recovery in selected patients.

Long-term management depends upon the underlying cause.

Complications

The major complication is recurrent disease or progression to multiple sclerosis.

Red Flags & Referral Guidance

Urgent assessment is required for:

  • Painful visual loss
  • Reduced colour vision
  • Relative afferent pupillary defect

Clinical Pearl

Pain on eye movement is one of the most characteristic features of optic neuritis.

OSCE Tip

Always ask whether colours appear less vivid. Red desaturation is a classic examination clue.

Key Takeaway

Optic neuritis is an inflammatory optic neuropathy strongly associated with multiple sclerosis. Painful visual loss and colour desaturation are hallmark features.

Central Retinal Artery Occlusion

Definition

Acute obstruction of the central retinal artery causing retinal ischaemia.

Symptoms

Sudden painless monocular visual loss.

Signs

Pale retina and a cherry-red spot.

Management

Emergency assessment and stroke workup.

Referral

Immediate emergency referral.

Central retinal artery occlusion (CRAO) is often described as an "eye stroke". It occurs when blood flow through the central retinal artery is interrupted, resulting in sudden retinal ischaemia.

Because retinal tissue is highly metabolically active, irreversible damage can occur within a short period of time. CRAO therefore represents a true ophthalmic emergency.

History of the Disease

The characteristic appearance of retinal artery occlusion became recognisable following the invention of the ophthalmoscope. Over time, clinicians realised that many cases shared risk factors with cerebrovascular disease and embolic stroke.

Modern management increasingly treats CRAO as a neurovascular emergency requiring urgent multidisciplinary assessment.

Timeline

  • 1851 - Ophthalmoscope invented
  • Late 1800s - Cherry-red spot recognised
  • 1900s - Vascular mechanisms understood
  • Present day - Managed as an ocular stroke

Epidemiology

CRAO is uncommon but carries devastating visual consequences. It occurs most frequently in older adults and shares many risk factors with cardiovascular disease and stroke.

Most patients have underlying vascular pathology.

Risk Factors

Hypertension, diabetes mellitus, hyperlipidaemia, smoking and carotid artery disease all increase the risk of CRAO. Cardiac embolic disease and atrial fibrillation are also important causes.

In younger patients, vasculitis and hypercoagulable states should be considered.

Pathophysiology

The central retinal artery supplies the inner retinal layers. Occlusion results in sudden interruption of oxygen delivery, leading to retinal infarction.

The characteristic cherry-red spot occurs because the oedematous retina becomes pale while the fovea remains relatively unaffected due to its separate blood supply from the choroid.

Symptoms

Patients typically describe sudden, profound and painless loss of vision in one eye. Visual loss usually develops over seconds to minutes and is often severe.

Many patients can identify the exact moment symptoms began.

Signs

Examination may reveal

  • Profound reduction in visual acuity
  • Relative afferent pupillary defect
  • Pale retina
  • Cherry-red spot
  • Attenuated retinal arteries

Investigations

  • Visual acuity assessment
  • Fundoscopy
  • OCT
  • Carotid imaging
  • ECG
  • Echocardiography where indicated

Management

Management focuses on identifying and treating the underlying vascular cause. Although various acute interventions have been attempted, evidence remains limited.

Patients require urgent stroke assessment and optimisation of cardiovascular risk factors.

Complications

The principal complication is permanent visual loss. Neovascular glaucoma may rarely develop following severe retinal ischaemia.

CRAO also signals a significantly increased future risk of stroke and cardiovascular events.

Red Flags & Referral Guidance

Immediate referral is required for:

  • Sudden painless monocular visual loss
  • Relative afferent pupillary defect
  • Suspected retinal artery occlusion

Clinical Pearl

CRAO should be considered an ocular stroke until proven otherwise.

OSCE Tip

When presented with sudden painless visual loss, always include CRAO, retinal detachment and vitreous haemorrhage in your differential diagnosis.

Key Takeaway

CRAO is an ocular emergency characterised by sudden painless monocular visual loss and a classic cherry-red spot. Urgent assessment is essential because it carries major implications for both vision and future vascular risk.

Central Retinal Vein Occlusion

Definition

Obstruction of the central retinal vein causing retinal congestion, haemorrhage and visual loss.

Symptoms

Sudden painless monocular visual loss or blurred vision.

Signs

Widespread retinal haemorrhages, dilated tortuous veins and optic disc swelling.

Management

Management of complications, particularly macular oedema, and optimisation of cardiovascular risk factors.

Referral

Urgent ophthalmology referral.

Central retinal vein occlusion (CRVO) is one of the most common retinal vascular disorders and is often referred to as the venous equivalent of an eye stroke. The condition occurs when blood flow through the central retinal vein becomes obstructed, leading to retinal congestion, haemorrhage and oedema.

Visual outcomes vary considerably. Some patients experience only mild visual impairment, whereas others develop severe and permanent visual loss.

History of the Disease

Following the invention of the ophthalmoscope, clinicians recognised a characteristic retinal appearance consisting of widespread haemorrhages and dilated veins. Over time, the condition became linked to cardiovascular risk factors such as hypertension and diabetes.

The introduction of anti-VEGF therapy has significantly improved visual outcomes in patients who develop macular oedema.

Timeline

  • 1851 - Ophthalmoscope introduced
  • Late 1800s - Retinal vein occlusions recognised
  • 1900s - Association with vascular disease established
  • 2000s - Anti-VEGF therapy introduced
  • Present day - Effective treatment for macular oedema available

Epidemiology

CRVO is most commonly seen in older adults and shares many risk factors with cardiovascular disease. The incidence increases with age and is strongly associated with hypertension, diabetes and hyperlipidaemia.

Although less common than diabetic retinopathy or AMD, CRVO remains an important cause of visual impairment.

Risk Factors

Hypertension is the most important risk factor. Additional associations include diabetes mellitus, hyperlipidaemia, smoking, glaucoma and cardiovascular disease.

Younger patients may require investigation for hypercoagulable states and systemic inflammatory disorders.

Pathophysiology

The central retinal vein passes through the optic nerve alongside the central retinal artery. Compression of the vein or thrombus formation may obstruct venous drainage from the retina.

As venous pressure rises, retinal haemorrhages, oedema and ischaemia develop. Severe retinal ischaemia stimulates VEGF release, which may result in neovascularisation and secondary complications.

Classification

CRVO is generally divided into:

Non-ischaemic CRVO

The more common and less severe form. Visual prognosis is generally better.

Ischaemic CRVO

A more severe subtype associated with extensive retinal non-perfusion, poor visual outcomes and a higher risk of neovascular complications.

Symptoms

Patients usually present with painless monocular visual loss. Vision may become blurred over hours or days and severity varies depending on the extent of retinal involvement.

Unlike retinal detachment, flashes and floaters are usually absent.

Signs

Examination may reveal

  • Reduced visual acuity
  • Dilated tortuous retinal veins
  • Widespread retinal haemorrhages
  • Cotton wool spots
  • Macular oedema
  • Optic disc swelling

The classic fundoscopic appearance is often described as a "blood and thunder" retina.

Investigations

  • Visual acuity testing
  • Fundoscopy
  • Optical coherence tomography (OCT)
  • Fluorescein angiography
  • Blood pressure measurement
  • Cardiovascular risk assessment

Management

Management focuses on treating complications and reducing future vascular risk. Anti-VEGF injections are commonly used to treat macular oedema and improve vision.

Patients should also undergo optimisation of cardiovascular risk factors, including blood pressure, cholesterol and diabetic control.

Complications

Complications include macular oedema, permanent visual loss, retinal neovascularisation, vitreous haemorrhage and neovascular glaucoma.

Neovascular glaucoma is one of the most feared complications and may occur several months after the initial event.

Red Flags & Referral Guidance

Urgent referral is required for:

  • Sudden painless visual loss
  • Suspected retinal vein occlusion
  • Neovascularisation
  • Marked visual deterioration

Clinical Pearl

The phrase "blood and thunder retina" is classically associated with CRVO.

OSCE Tip

When discussing sudden painless visual loss, always include both CRAO and CRVO in your differential diagnosis.

Key Takeaway

CRVO is a retinal vascular occlusion characterised by painless monocular visual loss and widespread retinal haemorrhages. Early ophthalmic assessment and management of complications are essential.

Giant Cell Arteritis

Definition

A large-vessel vasculitis affecting medium and large arteries, particularly the temporal arteries.

Symptoms

Headache, scalp tenderness, jaw claudication and visual symptoms.

Signs

Temporal artery tenderness and visual loss.

Management

Immediate corticosteroids.

Referral

Same-day emergency assessment.

Giant cell arteritis (GCA) is a medical emergency because it can cause irreversible visual loss if not recognised promptly. The condition results from inflammation of medium and large arteries, leading to vessel narrowing and reduced blood flow.

Ophthalmologists are particularly concerned because involvement of the posterior ciliary arteries can result in anterior ischaemic optic neuropathy and sudden blindness.

History of the Disease

The condition was first described in the nineteenth century but became more clearly defined during the twentieth century as advances in pathology demonstrated characteristic granulomatous inflammation within affected arteries.

Recognition of corticosteroid responsiveness transformed outcomes and dramatically reduced rates of bilateral blindness.

Timeline

  • 1890 - First clinical descriptions
  • 1930s - Histological features recognised
  • 1950s - Corticosteroids become standard treatment
  • Present day - Fast-track GCA pathways established

Epidemiology

GCA almost exclusively affects individuals over the age of fifty and is most common in people of Northern European ancestry.

Women are affected more frequently than men.

Risk Factors

Increasing age is the strongest risk factor. The condition is also associated with polymyalgia rheumatica, which coexists in many patients.

Pathophysiology

Inflammation within arterial walls causes intimal thickening and luminal narrowing. This reduces blood flow to tissues supplied by the affected vessels.

When the posterior ciliary arteries are involved, ischaemia of the optic nerve may occur, leading to sudden and irreversible visual loss.

Symptoms

Patients often present with a new temporal headache and scalp tenderness. Many report discomfort when brushing their hair or resting their head on a pillow.

Jaw claudication is particularly important and should always raise suspicion for GCA. Visual symptoms may include transient visual loss, diplopia or permanent blindness.

Systemic symptoms such as weight loss, fatigue and fever may also occur.

Signs

Examination may reveal

  • Temporal artery tenderness
  • Thickened temporal artery
  • Reduced visual acuity
  • Relative afferent pupillary defect
  • Pale swollen optic disc

Investigations

Assessment commonly includes:

  • ESR
  • CRP
  • Full blood count
  • Temporal artery ultrasound
  • Temporal artery biopsy

Investigations should never delay treatment.

Management

High-dose corticosteroids should be started immediately if GCA is suspected. Treatment should not be delayed while awaiting investigation results.

Long-term management is usually undertaken with rheumatology involvement.

Complications

The most feared complication is irreversible blindness. Stroke and aortic aneurysm formation may also occur.

Red Flags & Referral Guidance

Immediate assessment is required for:

  • New headache in an older patient
  • Jaw claudication
  • Visual symptoms
  • Elevated inflammatory markers

Clinical Pearl

Jaw claudication is one of the most specific clues to GCA.

OSCE Tip

Any patient over fifty with headache and visual symptoms should trigger consideration of GCA.

Key Takeaway

GCA is a sight-threatening vasculitis that requires immediate corticosteroid treatment to prevent irreversible blindness.

Chemical Eye Injury

Definition

Ocular damage caused by exposure to acidic or alkaline substances.

Symptoms

Severe pain, redness, watering and visual disturbance.

Signs

Conjunctival injection, corneal damage and ocular surface injury.

Management

Immediate copious irrigation.

Referral

Emergency ophthalmology assessment.

Chemical eye injury is one of the few true ophthalmic emergencies in which treatment must begin immediately, even before a full examination is performed. The severity of injury depends upon the chemical involved, its concentration and the duration of contact with the ocular surface.

Alkali injuries are particularly dangerous because they penetrate rapidly into ocular tissues and can cause extensive damage. Common examples include ammonia, bleach and cement.

Prompt irrigation can dramatically improve outcomes and should never be delayed.

History of the Disease

Occupational chemical injuries have been recognised for centuries. Industrialisation increased the incidence of severe ocular burns, leading to the development of emergency irrigation protocols that remain the cornerstone of management today.

Modern workplace safety measures have reduced the frequency of severe injuries, although chemical burns continue to occur in both occupational and domestic settings.

Timeline

  • 1800s - Industrial chemical injuries increasingly recognised
  • 1900s - Irrigation established as emergency treatment
  • Late 1900s - Standardised emergency protocols developed
  • Present day - Immediate irrigation remains the cornerstone of management

Epidemiology

Chemical injuries account for a significant proportion of ocular trauma presentations. Young working-age adults are most commonly affected, particularly in industrial environments.

Many injuries are preventable through the use of protective eyewear.

Risk Factors

Occupational exposure, laboratory work, household cleaning products and industrial chemicals all increase risk.

Failure to wear appropriate eye protection remains a major contributing factor.

Pathophysiology

Chemical agents damage the ocular surface through direct tissue injury.

Acid burns generally cause protein coagulation, which may limit deeper penetration. Alkali burns saponify cell membranes and penetrate rapidly into ocular tissues, often causing more severe injury.

Damage may involve the cornea, conjunctiva, limbal stem cells and deeper ocular structures.

Symptoms

Patients typically present with sudden severe pain, watering, redness and photophobia immediately after exposure.

Visual acuity may be reduced depending on the severity of injury.

A clear history of chemical exposure is often present.

Signs

Examination may reveal

  • Conjunctival injection
  • Corneal haze
  • Corneal epithelial defects
  • Conjunctival blanching
  • Limbal ischaemia
  • Reduced visual acuity

Investigations

The first priority is irrigation, not investigation.

Following stabilisation, assessment may include:

  • Visual acuity testing
  • Ocular pH measurement
  • Fluorescein staining
  • Slit lamp examination

Ocular pH should be checked repeatedly until normal.

Management

Immediate irrigation is the most important step in management.

Copious irrigation should begin immediately using any available clean fluid and continue until ocular pH normalises. Contact lenses should be removed where possible.

Following irrigation, patients require urgent ophthalmology assessment. Additional treatment may include lubricants, topical antibiotics, cycloplegics and anti-inflammatory therapy.

Complications

Severe injuries may result in corneal scarring, limbal stem cell deficiency, secondary glaucoma, cataract, corneal perforation and permanent visual loss.

Red Flags & Referral Guidance

Immediate emergency referral is required for:

  • Any chemical eye injury
  • Alkali exposure
  • Reduced vision
  • Corneal opacity
  • Limbal ischaemia

Treatment should begin before referral.

Clinical Pearl

The answer is always irrigate first. No investigation or examination is more important than immediate irrigation.

OSCE Tip

If presented with a chemical eye injury station, your first sentence should be: "I would immediately begin copious irrigation of the affected eye."

Key Takeaway

Chemical eye injury is an ophthalmic emergency. Immediate irrigation is the most important intervention and should begin as soon as possible, particularly following alkali exposure.
