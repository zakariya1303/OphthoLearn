10 Steps to a Wonderful Ophthalmic History

A structured approach to assessing patients with visual symptoms, ocular discomfort, or suspected eye disease.

Core principle

A good ophthalmic history often provides the diagnosis before you even pick up an ophthalmoscope.

A relatively simple symptom such as a painful red eye or a sudden change in vision can represent a genuine sight-threatening emergency. For this reason, ophthalmology places enormous emphasis on careful history taking.

Step 1: Beginning the Consultation

As with any patient interaction, begin by introducing yourself, confirming the patient's identity, and establishing the reason for attendance.

"Hello, my name is Zakariya. I'm a medical student working with the ophthalmology team today."

Many patients presenting with visual symptoms are understandably anxious. Vision is one of our most valued senses and even relatively minor symptoms can cause significant distress. A calm and reassuring manner therefore goes a long way in establishing rapport.

Even before asking questions, useful information can often be gathered through simple observation. Is the patient struggling to navigate the room? Are they wearing glasses? Is there obvious redness, swelling, watering, or discharge? Do they appear uncomfortable in bright light?

Step 2: Presenting Complaint

Start with an open question and allow the patient to tell their story.

"What has brought you in today?"

Patients frequently reveal the most important diagnostic clues within the first few sentences.

A patient who says, "I've had blurred vision for years and it's slowly getting worse", is describing a very different clinical picture to someone who says, "I woke up this morning and suddenly couldn't see properly out of one eye."

The first suggests a chronic process such as cataract or glaucoma. The second immediately raises concern for conditions such as retinal detachment, optic neuritis, or vitreous haemorrhage.

Establishing a timeline early is key. This will help you narrow your differential diagnosis rapidly and identify red flags requiring urgent referral.

Step 3: Exploring the History of Presenting Complaint

You should establish whether symptoms affect one eye or both. Many serious ophthalmic conditions initially affect only one eye. Patients may occasionally struggle to determine this themselves, particularly if the unaffected eye compensates well. Asking them to cover one eye at a time can sometimes help clarify the situation.

Understanding Visual Symptoms

Patients often describe visual symptoms in different ways, so it is important to clarify exactly what they mean.

Some patients simply describe blurred vision, but this can represent a huge range of pathology. Ask them to explain exactly what they are experiencing:

  • Is everything generally blurred?
  • Are straight lines appearing bent or distorted?
  • Is there a dark patch in the centre of vision?
  • Has part of their peripheral vision disappeared?
  • Do they feel as though a curtain or shadow is descending across their sight?

Each description points towards different pathology.

Distortion of straight lines, known as metamorphopsia, often suggests macular disease such as age-related macular degeneration.

A curtain-like shadow descending across vision is a classic description of retinal detachment and should always raise concern.

A central blind spot may indicate macular pathology, while progressive peripheral field loss is characteristic of chronic glaucoma.

Visual loss associated with eye movement pain raises concern for optic neuritis, particularly in younger patients. This is important because optic neuritis may represent the first presentation of multiple sclerosis.

Step 4: Flashes and Floaters

Patients should specifically be asked about flashes and floaters, even if they do not mention them spontaneously.

Flashes occur when traction is applied to the retina and are commonly described as brief flickers of light. Floaters appear as moving spots, cobwebs, or shadows drifting across vision.

Although these symptoms are often caused by a benign posterior vitreous detachment, they may also indicate retinal tears or retinal detachment.

The combination of new flashes, new floaters, and a curtain-like visual defect should always be treated as an ophthalmic emergency until proven otherwise.

Step 5: Pain

Pain is one of the most useful discriminating symptoms in ophthalmology.

Many serious retinal conditions cause profound visual loss but are completely painless. Conversely, conditions affecting the cornea, sclera, iris, or intraocular pressure are often extremely painful.

Determine if the eye is red and whether there is pain. This combination can rapidly narrow your differential diagnosis.

Ask about the severity, duration, and nature of the pain:

  • A mild gritty sensation may suggest dry eye disease or conjunctivitis.
  • Severe pain associated with photophobia may indicate keratitis or anterior uveitis.
  • A deep boring pain radiating into the forehead or jaw raises suspicion for scleritis.
  • Pain accompanied by headache, nausea and vomiting should immediately prompt consideration of acute angle-closure glaucoma.

Step 6: Associated Symptoms

Associated symptoms often provide crucial diagnostic clues and should be systematically explored.

Photophobia

Photophobia is particularly important because it frequently indicates corneal or intraocular inflammation. Conditions such as keratitis and anterior uveitis commonly present this way.

Systemic Symptoms

When a patient reports recurrent episodes of anterior uveitis, it is worth exploring symptoms suggestive of underlying systemic disease. Back pain, morning stiffness, psoriasis, inflammatory bowel disease, and joint problems may point towards a seronegative spondyloarthropathy such as ankylosing spondylitis.

Headaches

Headaches should be explored carefully. A new headache in an older patient accompanied by visual symptoms raises concern for giant cell arteritis. Ask specifically about scalp tenderness and jaw claudication, as these are important clues that may help prevent irreversible blindness.

Diplopia

Binocular diplopia often reflects ocular misalignment or cranial nerve pathology, whereas monocular diplopia is more commonly related to ocular disease such as cataract.

Step 7: Medical History

Systemic Diseases

Many systemic diseases have important ophthalmic manifestations.

  • Diabetes: Can lead to diabetic retinopathy and macular oedema.
  • Hypertension: Contributes to retinal vascular disease.
  • Autoimmune conditions: May present with uveitis, scleritis, or retinal vasculitis.
  • Thyroid disease: Can cause thyroid eye disease.
  • Neurological conditions: Such as multiple sclerosis may present with optic neuritis.

When appropriate, ask broader questions that help identify these associations. Sometimes the eye is the first clue that a patient has an underlying systemic illness.

Ocular History

Previous eye disease frequently provides valuable clues regarding current symptoms. Ask about:

  • Previous cataracts, glaucoma, diabetic retinopathy, or retinal detachments
  • Laser procedures, injections, or eye surgery
  • Current use of pressure-lowering drops or other ophthalmic medications

A history of retinal detachment increases the risk of future retinal pathology.

Contact Lens Use

Contact lens use deserves special attention. A painful red eye in a contact lens wearer should always raise concern for microbial keratitis—one of the most important red flags in ophthalmology because delayed treatment can rapidly result in corneal scarring and permanent visual loss.

Ask about lens hygiene, overnight lens wear, and whether lenses have been exposed to tap water.

Step 8: Drug History

Drug history is particularly important in ophthalmology because many medications affect the eye.

  • Long-term corticosteroid use increases the risk of cataracts and glaucoma.
  • Anticoagulants may influence bleeding risk and contribute to ocular haemorrhage.
  • Certain medications can cause dry eyes, blurred vision, or visual disturbances.
  • Eye drops themselves should never be forgotten. Many patients will know the colour of their drops but not the name, so additional clarification is often needed.

Always ask about allergies, particularly if treatment with topical medication is likely.

Step 9: Social History

Impact on Quality of Life

Visual symptoms can have a major impact on independence and quality of life. Understanding how symptoms affect daily activities often provides insight into disease severity and urgency.

Occupation

Ask about occupation because certain visual problems may significantly affect a person's ability to work safely. This context helps determine urgency of treatment.

Driving Status

Driving status is particularly important. Patients should be able to meet the legal visual standard for driving. Significant visual loss may have important implications for both driving safety and DVLA notification requirements.

2026 DVLA Standards: As of 2026, drivers must be able to read a number plate from 20 metres away; have visual acuity of at least 0.5 (6/12) on the Snellen scale (with glasses or contact lenses if necessary) using both eyes together, or in the remaining eye if sight in one eye only; and maintain an adequate field of vision. (Reference: gov.uk/driving-eyesight-rules, 2026)

Smoking History

Smoking is one of the strongest modifiable risk factors for age-related macular degeneration and contributes significantly to vascular disease throughout the body, including retinal vascular occlusions.

Step 10: Red Flags

Certain symptoms should immediately raise concern for sight-threatening pathology.

  • Sudden visual loss.
  • A painful red eye with reduced vision.
  • A contact lens wearer presenting with a red eye.
  • New flashes and floaters.
  • A curtain-like visual defect.
  • Chemical injury.
  • Visual symptoms associated with jaw claudication or temporal headache.

These presentations require urgent ophthalmic assessment and should never be dismissed as minor complaints.

About This Module

Target Audience: This module is specifically designed for ophthalmology F1s and F2s as well as medical students preparing for the OSCE examination.

We recognise that not all of the material covered in this comprehensive guide may be feasible to elicit or demonstrate within the constraints of a typical OSCE station. However, we believe it is valuable for learners to develop an appreciation of the breadth and depth of ophthalmic history-taking. Understanding these elements will enhance your clinical reasoning, improve patient rapport, and prepare you for real-world practice where comprehensive history-taking is essential.

Use this as a reference to deepen your understanding and refine your approach to ophthalmic consultations, both in examination settings and beyond.
